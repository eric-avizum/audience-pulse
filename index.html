Executive Summary

Fifty-four pharma intelligence professionals walked into a room. Ninety-six per cent of them told us the same thing: we see the signals, but we cannot act on them fast enough. That is not a rounding quirk. That is a room-wide confession.

The sensing scores are strong. This audience rates itself 4.3 out of 7 on spotting external opportunities, detecting fundamental shifts, and anticipating market changes. These are people who track GLP-1 disruption, cell and gene therapy economics, and IRA pricing reform. They are paying attention. The radar is on.

The velocity score is 3.7. The gap between what this room sees and what it acts on is 0.6 points on a 7-point scale. The radar is on but not connected to the cockpit. Day and Schoemaker found the same pattern in their 2019 study of 118 senior executives — leadership posture, not analytical capability, was the strongest predictor of whether sensing translated into action. This room is living that finding in real time.

Company size does not save you. The gap is 0.0 at firms with fewer than 500 people and 0.46 at firms with more than 50,000. More resources did not produce faster action. But seniority tells a different story. Senior Directors report the narrowest gap at 1.33, while Managers and Senior Managers report 0.75. VPs land at 0.0. The people closest to the decision table translate signals faster — not because they see more, but because they have shorter paths to action. For everyone else, intelligence remains a spectator sport.

The uncomfortable implication: the bottleneck is not capability. It is organisational distance. Shorten the path from signal to decision-maker and the gap closes. Leave it long and it does not matter how good your sensing is.

One recommendation for this room: stop asking whether your organisation detects signals well enough. It does. Start asking who has the authority to act on them within 48 hours — and whether that person is in the room where the signal first appears.
